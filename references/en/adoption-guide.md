# Adoption Guide

This guide is for taking `create-jarvis-skill` into a real company and getting from abstract interest to a usable pilot.

## The goal of adoption

The goal is not to impress people with a generated repository.

The goal is to create the first visible proof that:
- agents can find the right company context,
- agents can participate in real delivery work,
- and the organization can keep what they learn.

## Recommended adoption sequence

### 1. Start with one painful loop

Choose a loop that:
- happens often,
- crosses more than one surface,
- and hurts enough that people will notice improvement.

Good examples:
- recurring bug triage
- feature delivery across frontend/backend/QA/docs
- release-note generation from real engineering work

Avoid starting with a grand “map the whole company” ambition.

### 2. Pick a narrow but real scope

For the pilot, define:
- a few core sources,
- a few core repos,
- one real workflow,
- and the people who currently own that work.

This keeps the pilot grounded in real execution rather than architecture theater.

### 3. Build only what the pilot needs

Generate:
- the build brief,
- inventories,
- first-pass backlog,
- ownership map,
- rollout plan,
- and only the highest-leverage skill stubs.

### 4. Force adaptation before claiming success

Replace placeholders with:
- real owners,
- real repos,
- real systems,
- real access paths,
- and real workflow boundaries.

If the pilot still reads like a template, it is not ready.

### 5. Prove the loop

Use the pilot to complete real work.

Look for proof that the pilot reduced:
- rediscovery cost,
- context handoff cost,
- and cross-repo waiting cost.

### 6. Write back the lessons

After the pilot, update:
- JARVIS materials,
- the skill backlog,
- the ownership map,
- and the method itself if the rollout revealed a better pattern.

## What to expect in early adoption

Early rollouts are usually uneven.

Common realities:
- some systems are easy to connect and others are political
- repo-local guidance is usually missing or inconsistent
- ownership is often fuzzy until the pilot forces clarity
- people understand the value only after the first loop works

This is normal.

## A simple adoption test

The pilot is working when a second person or second agent can use the generated outputs to continue the loop without starting from zero.
